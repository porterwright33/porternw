What Two Years of Knocking Doors Taught Me About People

Lessons from the field that no classroom can give you

Posted by Porter Wright on April 1, 2026

Before I ever knocked on a door selling pest control, I spent two years knocking on doors in Trinidad, Tobago, and Guyana as a missionary. Thousands of conversations with strangers. Hundreds of rejections. A few that changed both of our lives. By the time I started my sales career with Vantage Marketing in Orlando, I had already been through a crash course in human nature that most salespeople never get.

Here is what I learned.

People Buy From People They Trust, Not People They Like

The first thing most people tell you in sales is to be likable. Smile. Mirror their energy. Build rapport. And while none of that is wrong, I found out pretty quickly that likability without credibility is just a nice conversation that ends in a polite "no thanks." What actually moves people is trust — the sense that you know what you're talking about, that you have their interest in mind, and that you will follow through on what you say.

I tested this in the field. Some of my best days were conversations where I was direct and even blunt about what the product could and couldn't do. Customers respected it. The ones I oversold to get the deal — those were the ones who cancelled later.

The Door Is Never Really Closed

"No" almost never means never. More often it means "not right now," "I don't understand the value," or "I don't feel safe saying yes yet." In Trinidad I used to see this clearly — families who turned us away three visits in a row would eventually become the most engaged people in the entire community. Persistence, delivered with respect and genuine care, changes things over time.

In sales, this plays out the same way. Some of my best closes came after a second or third visit, after a follow-up call, after I took the time to actually understand what was holding someone back. The door is almost never permanently closed. Most of the time it is just waiting for the right key.

"The best salespeople are not closers. They are openers — people who open conversations, open doors, and open minds."

What I Carry Forward

I am now studying business at BYU with plans to go into entrepreneurship or business development. The technical skills — finance, accounting, strategy — those I am building in school. But the ability to walk up to a stranger, earn their trust in five minutes, understand what they actually need, and communicate clearly under pressure? That came from years of doing it in the real world.

If you are a student thinking about direct sales, I would not tell everyone to do it. It is hard. It is uncomfortable. Some days are genuinely demoralizing. But for the right person, at the right time, there is no faster education in people, persuasion, and resilience. I would not trade it for anything.
